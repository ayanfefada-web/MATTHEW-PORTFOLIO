Power BI Analytics Portfolio

Author: M.A. Oyeyiola

This repository showcases a set of independent Power BI dashboards covering employee attrition, leavers, compensation & retention, general HR metrics, and company financial performance. Each dashboard was built as its own standalone analysis with its own dataset, business questions, and executive summary. They are presented together here as a portfolio, in the order they were produced.

---

1. Employee Attrition Analysis — Executive Summary

A narrative write-up of the attrition dashboard below, translating the numbers into three business stories: Premium Talent Drain (the company is losing experienced mid-tier staff, not entry-level employees, concentrated in the $60k–$150k salary range), Critical R&D and Marketing Vulnerability (Marketing and Engineering post above-average attrition, with R&D carrying the largest overall share), and Demographic and Geographic Friction Points (attrition skews male and disproportionately affects Black and Asian employees, with China as the highest-attrition country). Each story comes with its own recommended actions — stay interviews, salary-structure reviews, leadership assessments, and DEI initiatives.

---

2. Attrition Rate Analysis — Page 1

The main attrition dashboard. KPI cards show an 8.56% attrition rate across 911 employees (833 active, 78 leavers) with an average tenure of 4.8 years before leaving. Visuals break attrition down by department (Marketing and Engineering highest), by gender (male higher than female), by ethnicity (Black and Asian employees disproportionately affected), and by year, with a filterable department list.

---

3. Attrition Rate Analysis — Page 2

The second page of the same dashboard, going deeper: attrition by business unit (R&D carries the largest share), by job title (Business Partners, Network Engineers, and Operations roles show the highest counts), by salary group (attrition peaks in the $60k–$150k range and drops sharply above $200k), and by country (China highest, followed by Brazil and the U.S.), with a country and business unit filter.

---

4. Employee Salary and Retention Analysis

A separate dashboard connecting compensation to retention. KPI cards show an average salary of $113.34K, a retention rate of 91.44%, and an attrition rate of 8.6%. Visuals cover salary by department, attrition by bonus category (no-bonus employees churn the most), retention by department (IT retains best despite lower pay; Marketing and Engineering retain worst despite higher pay), a salary-vs-tenure trend showing pay peaking mid-career then declining for the most senior staff, leavers by bonus category, and attrition rate by salary level, with department and salary-group filters.

---

5. Employee Retention — Executive Summary

The narrative companion to the salary and retention dashboard above. It surfaces a No-Bonus Retention Risk (9.3% attrition among unbonused staff, with 45 of the 78 leavers falling into this group, versus just 4.5% for low-bonus staff), a Mid-Career Compensation Decline (salary drops for employees after 20–30 years of service, with some veterans earning less than new hires), and Marketing and Engineering Retention Challenges (both receive strong salary investment but retain worse than IT). Recommended actions include expanding low-bonus eligibility, auditing long-tenure compensation, and strengthening retention incentives in Engineering and Marketing.

---

6. Financial Analysis Dashboard

An entirely separate project analyzing overall company financial performance rather than HR data. KPI cards show total profit of $17M, total cost of $102M, a profit margin of 14.23%, total sales of $119M, gross sales of $127.93M, and a profit percentage of 16.59%. Visuals break profit down by country, by product (Paseo leads, Carretera and Montana lag), by year and month (highly volatile, swinging between roughly $0.5M and $2M), and by customer segment (Government dominates at $11M; Enterprise is the only segment operating at a loss), with product and country filters.

---

7. Financial Analysis — Executive Summary

The narrative write-up for the financial dashboard above, framed as an executive financial health check. It flags the Enterprise segment's -$1M loss (likely from over-discounting or scope creep), extreme month-to-month profit volatility, a concentration risk with profit leaning heavily on the Government segment and the Paseo product, and a high cost structure consuming over 85% of revenue. Recommended actions: audit and renegotiate Enterprise contracts, cut supply chain costs (a 5% reduction alone would save an estimated $5.1M), secure more multi-year Government contracts, and review production costs on lagging products.

---

8. Leavers Analysis — Executive Summary

A narrative summary of the leavers dashboard below, built entirely around the 78 employees who actually left. It highlights losing experienced leaders (Directors and Senior Managers post the highest departure counts, many with no bonus), R&D and Engineering pressure (the highest attrition levels, especially in the United States, suggesting burnout or workload issues), and mid-level career growth gaps (high turnover among Managers, Senior Analysts, and Business Analysts pointing to succession-planning challenges).

---

9. Leavers Analysis

The dashboard behind the summary above. A KPI card shows 78 total leavers, broken down by country (United States far in the lead with 47, then China and Brazil), gender (a near-even 43 male vs. 35 female split), department (Engineering, Marketing, and IT top the list), job title (Directors and Senior Managers highest), bonus category (58% of leavers received no bonus at all), and business unit (Research & Development and Manufacturing lead), with a department filter.

---

10. HR Data Overview

A standalone, high-level HR snapshot rather than an attrition-focused view. KPI cards show 926 total staff across 12 departments and a $74M total salary spend. Visuals cover headcount by department (Training is the largest at 101 employees, 55.38% ahead of Marketing, the smallest at 65), salary by gender (male accounts for 50.16% of spend), headcount by gender (49.14% male, 46.54% female, 4.32% intersex), and salary by department (Training also leads in spend at $8.3M), with department and gender filters and an auto-generated report summary panel.
